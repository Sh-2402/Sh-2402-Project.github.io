Ireland

About

Ireland is an island in the North Atlantic Ocean, in Northwestern Europe. Geopolitically, the island is divided between the Republic of Ireland (officially named Ireland – a sovereign state covering five-sixths of the island) and Northern Ireland (part of the United Kingdom – covering the remaining sixth). It is separated from Great Britain to its east by the North Channel, the Irish Sea, and St George's Channel. Ireland is the second-largest island of the British Isles, the third-largest in Europe, and the twentieth-largest in the world. As of 2022, the population of the entire island is just over 7 million, with 5.1 million in the Republic of Ireland and 1.9 million in Northern Ireland, ranking it the second-most populous island in Europe after Great Britain.

Culture

Ireland's culture comprises elements of the culture of ancient peoples, later immigrant and broadcast cultural influences (chiefly Gaelic culture, Anglicisation, Americanisation and aspects of broader European culture). In broad terms, Ireland is regarded as one of the Celtic nations of Europe, alongside Scotland, Wales, Cornwall, Isle of Man and Brittany. This combination of cultural influences is visible in the intricate designs termed Irish interlace or Celtic knotwork. These can be seen in the ornamentation of medieval religious and secular works. The style is still popular today in jewellery and graphic art, as is the distinctive style of traditional Irish music and dance, and has become indicative of modern "Celtic" culture in general.

Sports

Gaelic football is the most popular sport in Ireland in terms of match attendance and community involvement, with about 2,600 clubs on the island. In 2003 it represented 34% of total sports attendances at events in Ireland and abroad, followed by hurling at 23%, soccer at 16% and rugby at 8%. The All-Ireland Football Final is the most watched event in the sporting calendar. Soccer is the most widely played team game on the island and the most popular in Northern Ireland.

Sporting activities with the highest levels of playing participation include swimming, golf, aerobics, cycling, and billiards/snooker. Many other sports are also played and followed, including boxing, cricket, fishing, greyhound racing, handball, hockey, horse racing, motor sport, show jumping and tennis.

Things to do in Ireland

Some must-do experiences around the island.

Cliffs of Moher

Walk along the dramatic Atlantic coastline.

Explore Dublin

Stroll Temple Bar, the Liffey, and city cafes.

Hike to the highest point in Ireland

The strenuous hike to the 1,038m peak of Carrauntoohil in Kerry is worth the effort.

Night in Galway

Live music, colourful streets and cosy pubs.

Packing

Dress in layers: The weather can change quickly, so pack clothes that can be layered to adapt to sun, wind, and rain.
Invest in waterproof gear: A good raincoat, rain pants, and waterproof or water-resistant shoes are highly recommended.
Bring a reusable water bottle: Tap water is safe to drink in both the Republic and Northern Ireland.

Getting around

Driving: If you choose to drive, rent a car well in advance. Be aware that roads can be narrow and you will be driving on the left.
Public transport: Trains and tour buses are good alternatives if you prefer not to drive.

Cultural tips

Embrace the local culture: Don't be afraid to try local pubs and restaurants, which often feature live music. Get to know the locals, who are known for their friendliness.
Learn some slang: Familiarize yourself with some Irish slang to better understand conversations.
Know about pubs: Pubs are a central part of Irish culture, often serving as social hubs. A pint of Guinness is a must-try for many visitors.
